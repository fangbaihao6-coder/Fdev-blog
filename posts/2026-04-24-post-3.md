## Designing User Flow and Application Structure

After defining the core functional requirements in the previous stage, I started to think about how users will actually interact with the application. At this point, the focus shifted from “what the system does” to “how the user experiences it”.

Because this application is designed for recovery rather than general fitness, the user flow needs to be simple, clear, and guided. Users are likely to be in a situation where they are uncertain about their condition, so reducing confusion is an important design goal.

The first step in the user flow is onboarding. When users enter the app for the first time, they should not be presented with a large amount of content. Instead, they should be guided to identify their recovery context. This could include selecting their injury type, recovery stage, or current symptoms. This step is important because it allows the system to provide more relevant information later.

After onboarding, the user is directed to a personalised home page. Instead of a general feed, this page displays filtered content based on the user’s selected recovery stage. For example, users in early recovery may see basic movement guidance, while users in later stages may see strength-related content.

Another key part of the flow is the recovery log. Users should be able to record their daily condition, such as pain level or exercises completed. This feature is designed to support self-reflection and progress tracking. Over time, users can look back at their logs to understand how their condition has changed.

I also considered how users navigate between different parts of the app. To keep the structure simple, I am thinking of using a limited number of main sections:
- Home (guided content)
- Log (record progress)
- Profile (recovery information)

This reduces cognitive load and makes the application easier to use.

There is a trade-off in this structure. By limiting navigation options, the app becomes easier to understand, but it also reduces flexibility. For example, users may not be able to freely explore all content like they would in a traditional social platform. However, this limitation is intentional, as it supports the goal of reducing unnecessary information.

Another consideration is how much control the user should have. A fully guided system may feel restrictive, while a fully open system may feel overwhelming. At this stage, I am aiming for a balance, where users have some control but are still guided by the system.

Overall, this stage helped me understand that user flow is not just about navigation, but about shaping the user’s experience and decision-making process.

In the next stage, I plan to explore how data will be structured within the application, including how user information and recovery logs will be stored.
