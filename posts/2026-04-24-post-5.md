## Visual Design Decisions and Iteration

In the final stage of this project, our focus shifted towards visual design and evaluation. After confirming the overall structure and user flow, our team explored multiple visual directions before selecting a final style.

![Initial Wireframe](/assets/wireframe.png)

The original design was based on a simple grayscale wireframe, which focused on layout clarity rather than visual expression. While this helped define structure, it lacked identity and emotional engagement. As a result, we began exploring different visual styles.

We considered several alternative directions:

![Rejected Style 1](/assets/style-dark.png)

The first option used a dark, high-contrast interface inspired by modern dashboard designs. While visually appealing, it introduced complexity and reduced readability. The strong glow effects and layered visuals made it harder to quickly scan information, which is critical in a recovery context.

![Rejected Style 2](/assets/style-light.png)

The second option adopted a soft, minimal style with light colours and gradients. Although this version improved readability, it lacked visual distinction and did not strongly communicate the system’s identity. It felt too generic and did not align with the concept of a structured, guided recovery tool.

![Rejected Style 3](/assets/style-glass.png)

Another variation explored a more stylised interface with layered visual elements and decorative graphics. However, this approach increased visual noise and distracted from the core information. This conflicted with our goal of reducing cognitive load for users.

After comparing these options, we decided to adopt a pixel-inspired visual style:

![Final Pixel Style](/assets/pixel-style.png)
This direction was influenced by retro digital interfaces, early desktop systems, and game-inspired aesthetics such as Minecraft and classic Nintendo visuals. The pixel style creates a clear and structured layout, with strong visual hierarchy and consistent components. It also introduces a unique identity while maintaining clarity.

As part of my role, I was responsible for designing the icons, typography, and layout system. However, this version still has several limitations. For example, some text is not easily readable, and certain layout areas feel visually crowded. These issues highlight the trade-off between stylistic expression and usability.

In addition to visual design, I also focused on evaluation and ethical considerations. From an evaluation perspective, the main goal is to ensure that users can easily understand the interface and complete key tasks without confusion. This includes testing navigation clarity, readability, and the effectiveness of guided content.

From an ethical perspective, accessibility remains a key concern. The pixel style must still maintain sufficient contrast and readable font sizes. Additionally, since the application involves recovery-related information, it is important to minimise data collection and ensure that user information is handled responsibly.

Overall, this stage demonstrated that design is an iterative process. Our team moved from a basic wireframe, through multiple rejected visual styles, to a final direction that balances identity and usability. While the current design is not final, it provides a strong foundation for further refinement.
