## Revising Design Direction and Considering Evaluation & Visual Design

After developing the initial wireframes, our group held a discussion to compare different design approaches. During this process, we realised that our original wireframe, including the one I contributed to, did not fully support the guided recovery experience we aimed to achieve. As a result, the team decided to discard that version and adopt an alternative design proposed by another team member.

This decision was not based on visual preference, but on how well the design aligned with our functional requirements. The selected wireframe provided a clearer user flow and better reflected the idea of guiding users through recovery rather than allowing unrestricted browsing. This highlighted an important aspect of the design process: not all initial ideas are suitable, and iteration is necessary to improve alignment between concept and implementation.

After selecting the new wireframe direction, we began discussing how to transform it into a high-fidelity visual design. At this stage, the focus shifted from structure to presentation. We explored the possibility of using a more stylised visual direction inspired by modern digital products, including a darker interface with strong contrast and minimal but impactful colour accents. This approach is still under discussion, as we are trying to balance visual appeal with usability.

There is a clear trade-off in this decision. A more stylised interface can make the application feel more engaging and modern, but it may also reduce readability or accessibility if not designed carefully. Because this application is related to recovery, clarity and ease of use remain the priority. Visual design should support the user’s understanding, not distract from it.

As part of my role in the team, I am responsible for both evaluation and ethical considerations, as well as contributing to the visual design direction. From an evaluation perspective, I am focusing on how the application can be assessed once it is developed. This includes usability testing, where users will be observed completing key tasks such as onboarding and logging recovery data. It also includes checking whether users can easily understand the guided system without confusion.

In terms of ethical considerations, one important issue is how user data is handled. Since the app involves personal health-related information, it is important to minimise the amount of data collected and ensure that it is stored securely. Another consideration is accessibility. The visual design, especially if it adopts a darker or more stylised aesthetic, must still maintain sufficient contrast and readability for all users.

Overall, this stage showed that design is not a linear process. Decisions may change as ideas are tested and compared. The shift from our original wireframe to a new structure, and now towards a high-fidelity visual direction, reflects an ongoing process of refinement.

In the next stage, I plan to further develop evaluation methods and refine the visual design to ensure that it supports both usability and user trust.
